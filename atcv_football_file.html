Unveiling the Truth: Resolving Goal-Line Controversies with Computer Vision

Universitat Politècnica de Catalunya (UPC)

In high-stakes football matches, controversial moments like whether a ball crossed the goal line can spark heated debates. Despite the use of cutting-edge technologies like VAR (Video Assistant Referee), certain plays remain inconclusive due to issues like perspective distortion and occlusions. This project employs advanced computer vision techniques to resolve such a dispute, leveraging homography and 3D reconstruction to determine the exact position of the ball.

The first step was to choose suitable images for analysis. Out of several candidate images, four perspectives were selected based on their clarity and visibility of key features, such as the corners of the goal and the ball. Images with distortions or missing critical details were discarded to ensure accuracy.

To ensure consistent tracking of the ball, a red circle was overlaid on its position in each image. Next, the visible corners of the goal were identified, and for obscured areas, projections of visible lines were used to estimate their positions geometrically. This step was crucial for constructing an accurate 3D representation.

The core of the analysis involved computing homographies to map 2D image coordinates onto a common goal plane. Using the Direct Linear Transform (DLT) algorithm, homography matrices were derived for each image. These matrices enabled the transformation of the ball's position in the images onto the shared plane, ensuring alignment across perspectives.

To identify the plane where the ball was truly located, parallel planes were constructed inward and outward from the goal.

Iteratively, the plane where the ball’s centers from all images aligned most closely was determined as its actual position. After computing the homography of the ball for each imaginary plane -green/red- on each of the four considered images, the following results have been obtained.

In order to determine which of all the new planes is the actual ball plane, the distances between the centre projections have been computed during the iterations. These distance has been computed as the sum ofdistances between all the pair of centers (1-2, 1-3, 1-4, 2-3, 2-4, 3-4) . It has been obtained that the minimum distance between centers is obtained at the 21st plane, with a distance sum of 68.84 cm. After determining the ball's position, its 3D coordinates were calculated. A simulated 3D scenario was created to visualize the ball's depth and proximity to the goal line. The results revealed that the center of the ball had crossed the goal plane by a margin of 12.5 cm, confirming it was a valid goal.

As it can be observed on the image below, which is taken directly from above, perpendicular to the horizontal plane (bird's-eye view), the ball was inside the goal with a margin of 1,31cm (considering the radius of the ball of aprox 11,5cm).

This project demonstrates the power of computer vision in resolving ambiguous situations in sports. By combining homography and 3D modeling, the analysis provides a transparent and accurate resolution to the controversy using minimal resources, relying solely on the analysis of several 2D images taken simultaneously. The methodology is not only reproducible but also holds significant potential for enhancing current technologies like VAR
