Academic journey

I began my academic journey in Tarragona, Spain, where I completed a technological baccalaureate. Later, I moved to Barcelona to pursue a Bachelor’s degree in Industrial Technologies Engineering at UPC (Universitat Politècnica de Catalunya), ETSEIB. I remember this transition as one of the most challenging periods of my life — moving to a new city, adapting to a demanding academic environment — but I faced it with great motivation.

I truly enjoyed my studies, but I also felt the urge to specialize in a specific field — particularly in ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE and ROBOTICS. At the same time, I didn’t want to step away from my foundation in industrial engineering. That ambition led me to apply for the Double Master’s program in Industrial Engineering and Automatic Control & Robotics, also at UPC, ETSEIB. These have been formative years, where I’ve been able to dive deep into the topics that fascinate me most.

Most recently, I applied to carry out my Master’s Thesis at one of the most prestigious universities in Europe and the world: EPFL (École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne), in Switzerland. I still remember the moment I received the acceptance letter, which marked the beginning of one of my biggest personal and academic adventures — which I’m currently living. I’m now part of the DISAL lab, working on my thesis titled "Belief Sharing and Merging of a Multi-Robot System for Gas Source Localization Task", which is planned to be presented in late July 2025.

Interests

As a kid, I loved spending time drawing and crafting — activities that sparked my imagination and taught me the value of design. Years later, when I discovered how mathematics could be applied to images, I was fascinated. That moment opened the door to COMPUTER VISION, a field that felt like magic — turning raw pixels into understanding. It became clear to me: this was how robots could “see,” how they could begin to perceive the world like we do.

As I delved deeper into this world, I encountered ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE, and something clicked. I was amazed that something so abstract — statistical models, probabilities, data — could give rise to intelligent behavior. I didn’t just study it; I started building models on my own, outside of class, driven purely by curiosity. If Computer Vision gives robots eyes, AI gives them a brain.

But perception and reasoning weren’t enough for me — I wanted those ideas to move. That’s when ROBOTICS brought everything together. Combining my engineering background with my growing interest in perception and intelligence, I started designing systems that could act in the real world. Robotics gave me the hands to match the eyes and the brain, enabling interaction with the world.

To complete the picture, I explored algorithms for NAVIGATION AND LOCALIZATION, giving machines the ability to understand where they are, where they’re going, and how to get there efficiently. It’s the final piece of the puzzle — granting them freedom of movement and true autonomy in the physical world.

This journey hasn’t just shaped my academic interests — it’s shaped who I am. I’m passionate about solving meaningful problems, driven by a need to understand how things work, and motivated by the possibility of shaping a future where technologies improve lives.
